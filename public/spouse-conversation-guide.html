The Spouse Conversation Guide

Five Questions That Actually Move Things Forward

A companion to The Anti-Retirement Guide by Nick Constantinou

Most couples have the wrong conversation about retirement.

One person has been building a spreadsheet. The other keeps saying the numbers aren't ready, or the timing isn't right, or "let's just wait and see." And every time the conversation happens, it ends in the same place it started — one person frustrated, the other defensive, neither of them quite sure what they're actually arguing about.

The problem isn't the numbers. The numbers are usually fine. The problem is that one conversation is being used to carry two very different fears, and they're talking past each other without realising it.

This guide won't give you a script. Scripts don't work. What it will give you is five questions that change the shape of the conversation — away from the spreadsheet and toward what the conversation actually needs to be about.

Before You Start

One rule: don't have this conversation at home.

Home is where the to-do list lives. Where one of you will get distracted. Where you've already had versions of this conversation that didn't go anywhere. Go somewhere else — a walk, a coffee, somewhere neutral. It's not ceremonial. It just works.

And don't lead with the numbers. Not yet. Numbers come last, not first.

The Five Questions

Question 1

"If money was genuinely sorted — not roughly fine, actually sorted — what would you want your life to look like?"

Not what you'd tolerate. Not what seems realistic. What you'd actually want.

Give them time to answer. This question often produces a long silence, and the silence is not a problem. It means they're thinking about something they haven't been asked before.

What you're listening for: what they're moving toward, not what they're afraid of. Write it down if they'll let you.

Question 2

"What does retirement mean to you?"

This one sounds obvious. It isn't.

The word "retirement" carries completely different freight for different people. For some it means freedom. For others it means their parents' decline, or becoming invisible, or losing the thing that makes them worth something. Neither of you can have a useful conversation until you know what the word means to the other person.

Don't correct their answer. Don't say "but that's not what retirement has to be." Just listen.

Question 3

"What are you worried about that we haven't actually talked about yet?"

Not "what are your concerns about the plan." That question produces a list of practical objections — the mortgage, the children, the market. Some of those are real, but most of them are proxy fears.

This question asks for the thing underneath. The fear about who they'll be. About what happens to your marriage when the structure of your days disappears. About the version of you they haven't met yet.

It's a vulnerable question. Answer it yourself first — with your own fears, honestly. That makes it easier for them to answer.

Question 4

"Is there anything that would have to happen before you could feel ready?"

Not "when do you think you'll be ready" — that's a closed question with a defensive answer. This version is open. It assumes readiness is possible and asks what it would take.

Sometimes there's a milestone. A project finished, a promotion landed, a child settled. Sometimes it's more abstract — a feeling of security they can't quite put a number on. Whatever the answer is, it's information you can actually work with.

Question 5

"What would make you excited about this — not just okay with it, but actually excited?"

Most retirement conversations aim for agreement. This question aims for enthusiasm. It's a different target.

You may not get an answer right away. That's fine. Leave it open. "Have a think. There's no rush." And then actually mean it — don't follow up in three days. Let it sit.

The question plants something. It shifts the frame from "loss" to "possibility." Give it time.

How to Close the Conversation

Don't push for a decision. That's not what this conversation is for.

End with something like: "I don't need an answer now. I just wanted to hear what you actually think. And I wanted you to know what I'm thinking too."

Then stop talking. Let the conversation breathe.

The most useful thing that can come out of this isn't agreement — it's honesty. Two people who understand each other's actual fears are in a far better position than two people who've agreed on a timeline they're both secretly uncertain about.
