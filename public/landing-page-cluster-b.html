The Problem

Retiring solo is a different kind of challenge

Nobody warns you about the quiet. The sudden emptiness when the emails stop. The days stretching ahead with no one to answer to — and no one to share them with.

🔇

The Silence Hits Hard

After decades of meetings, deadlines, and colleagues, the house goes quiet. Your partner never existed or is no longer here. The commute you dreaded is suddenly the thing you miss most.

🏷️

Identity Without a Job Title

"What do you do?" has defined you for 40 years. When you answer "I'm retired," the conversation dies. And so, quietly, does a significant part of how you see yourself.

📋

No One to Plan With

Your friends are still working or travelling with their spouses. Planning anything feels pointless when there's no one to plan with. The future feels like a solo expedition with no map.

🎯

Purpose? What Purpose?

The pension is sorted. The mortgage is paid. So why does waking up at 7am with nothing to do feel less like freedom and more like abandonment?

FAQ

Solo retirement — answered honestly

I'm not lonely — I just need to figure out what to do with my days. Is this book for me?
Absolutely. Loneliness is only one facet of solo retirement. The book addresses purpose, identity, daily structure, and social connection — whether you identify as lonely or simply unoccupied. Most readers come for one issue and stay for the rest.
I'm naturally introverted. Do I have to become a "people person" to enjoy retirement?
Not at all. The Anti-Retirement Guide specifically addresses introverted solo seekers. It debunks the myth that meaningful connection requires extroversion. You'll find strategies for depth over breadth — fewer, richer relationships rather than a packed social calendar you dread.
I've already tried picking up hobbies. Nothing sticks. Why would this be different?
Hobbies are the symptom, not the cure. Most people try to solve a purpose problem with a leisure activity. This book works upstream — helping you understand your identity needs first, then building hobbies and connections that actually feel sustaining rather than obligatory.
I'm worried about being a burden if I reach out to old friends. How does the book handle this?
This comes up constantly, and the book addresses it directly. It includes practical scripts for reconnecting without awkwardness, reframes the "burden" narrative with evidence-based perspective shifts, and offers gentle step-by-step approaches for rebuilding social confidence at your own pace.
Is this just another self-help book full of platitudes?
No. The Anti-Retirement Guide is grounded in real transition science, behavioural psychology, and interviews with over 120 solo retirees across the UK. It's practical, honest, and sometimes uncomfortable — because the truth about building a meaningful solo retirement requires more than affirmations.
