What I Actually Want

12 Questions to Cut Through the Noise

A companion to The Anti-Retirement Guide by Nick Constantinou

You've been busy your whole career. Good at things. Recognised for it. Forward-moving.

And now — whether you've left already or you're planning to — the question that should be simple is somehow the hardest one you've faced: what do I actually want?

Not what looks good. Not what sounds purposeful when you say it out loud. Not the answer that proves you haven't gone soft or lost your edge.

What you actually want.

This is harder than it sounds because you've spent twenty or thirty years in environments that rewarded having the right answer. In those environments, "I don't know what I want" is a failure state. Here, it's the only honest starting point.

These twelve questions are designed to cut through the performance and get to something real. There are no right answers. There are only your answers.

Work through them slowly. One at a time. Some will land immediately. Some won't make sense until the third read. Write your answers down — not on a phone, on paper. The act of writing changes what comes out.

The Questions

1

When you picture a genuinely good day — not a productive day, not an impressive day, just a day you'd call good — what's in it? Be specific. What time do you wake up? What's the first thing you do?

 
 
 
2

What did you used to love doing before it became your job? Or before the job crowded it out?

 
 
3

What have you always been curious about but never had the time or the excuse to pursue? Not a passion. A curiosity. Something you'd think about on a slow Tuesday if nobody was watching.

 
 
4

What do people come to you for — not in your professional capacity, but just as a person? What do they trust you with?

 
 
5

If you had to teach something — not your job, something you know outside of it — what would it be? Who would you want in the room?

 
 
6

What are you avoiding thinking about? The thing that surfaces when you're in the shower or can't sleep — not the financial thing, the other thing. What is it?

 
 
7

Who in your life has built something after fifty that you actually admire? Not envied — admired. What did they build, and what was it about it that you noticed?

 
 
8

What would you do if you knew it wouldn't go anywhere? If there was no pitch, no funding round, no audience, no validation — just the thing itself. Would you still do it?

 
 
9

What kind of tired do you want to feel at the end of a day? Physically tired from something you built with your hands? Mentally tired from a problem you were genuinely engaged with? Emotionally tired from being present with people?

 
 
10

What's the story you tell yourself about why now isn't the right time? Look at it carefully. Is it true? Or is it a habit?

 
 
11

If you fast-forward five years: what do you want to have done, not what do you want to have? Not the account balance or the asset. The thing you did.

 
 
12

What's the smallest possible version of what you want that you could start this week — not plan, not research, not think about, but actually start?

 
 

What to Do With Your Answers

Don't try to build a strategy from them immediately. Let them sit for a day.

Then come back and look for the patterns. Which answers surprised you? Which made you uncomfortable? Which ones did you answer immediately without having to think?

The uncomfortable answers and the immediate ones are usually pointing at the same thing.

The Principle Underneath

Most people in your situation aren't short of possibilities. They're short of permission.

Permission to want something that isn't impressive. Permission to start something small. Permission to not know the destination before they take the first step.

These questions don't give you a plan. They give you information about what you actually want — which is the only foundation a plan is worth building on.
